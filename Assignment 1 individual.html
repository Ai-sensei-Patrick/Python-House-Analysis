def confidence_interval_welch(xbar1, var1, n1, xbar2, var2, n2, alpha):
    mean = xbar1 - xbar2
    std_mean = np.sqrt(var1 / n1 + var2 / n2)

    p1 = (var1 / n1 + var2 / n2) ** 2
    p2 = ((var1 / n1) ** 2 / (n1 - 1)) + ((var2 / n2) ** 2 / (n2 - 1))
    dof = p1 / p2

    tcrit = stats.t.ppf(1 - alpha / 2., dof)
    lower = mean - tcrit * std_mean
    upper = mean + tcrit * std_mean
    return lower, upper, dof

# this function computes the Welch two-sample t-test
confidence_interval_welch(1661876.0245398772,1255232816099.802,163,531563.5998135199,116690279653.07115,21450,0.05)

(956963.2928260339, 1303661.5566266805, 162.22896438139523)

The hypotheses are:

$H_0:$ the two group means are equal: $\mu_{\text{Waterfront}} = \mu_{\text{NoWaterfront}}$.

$H_1:$ the two group means are not equal: $\mu_{\text{Waterfront}} \neq \mu_{\text{Nowaterfront}}$.

$\alpha=0.05$ as standard.

The test stat is $t = 12.88$, which has a t distribution with approximately 162 degrees of freedom, if the null of equality of means is, and the assumptions are also, true. The chance that the resulting t value would be as high or higher than $12.88$ in absolute value is very close to 0, i.e. here the p-val is $P(|t_{162} \geq 12.88) = 1.38 \times 10^{-26}$. This is $< 0.05$, so I reject the null and conclude that the two group means are significantly different to each other. In other words, there is a significant relationship between price and house located in waterfront.

The test assumed that the data are iid in each group, the two groups are independent of each other and the 4th moment of the price in each group is finite. The first and second assumption, the price of the house simply relies on waterfront or non waterfront, there are no other features that effect the price of the house (size of the house or house location), although this assumption has not been proven yet. The 4th moment assumption will be satisfied if price is a bounded variable. Clearly it is bounded below by \$0; further, since number of waterfront houses and non waterfront houses are finite and each prices tag of the house are finite, I can regard price as implicitly bounded via these aspects, but the outlier is not "rare" for both two groups of data so the 4th moment assumption are not quiet satissfied in here.

In summary, T-test is right only when the outlier of both two groups are "rare" and there is no other feature that effect the house price.

$H_1:$ waterfront house price has a distribution shifted upwards compared to the non waterfront house price: $P(Y_1 > Y_2) > 0.5$.

I choose $\alpha = 0.05$ as standard.

The test statistic is $U = 3090831$, which under the null follows a large sample normal distribution with mean and variance as given by the test. In this case, the p-value is $P(U > 18903) = $$3.2430420782356034 \times 10^{-64}$, where the mean and variance are specified in the test. This is $< 0.05$, thus I reject the null hypothesis. I conclude that there is significant waterfront effect on the distribution of house price, which waterfront house price shifted upwards compared to non waterfront house price.

The assumptions made are:

I assume both two group 'waterfront' and 'non waterfront' are independent group to each other.

data are i.i.d. in each group, house price are independent between each house and other house.

each group's sample size is large enough for the CLT to work. Sample sizes of 163 and 21450 and easily high enough here.

data are at least on the ordinal scale: price is a ratio variable, so this is satisfied.

Based on the lecture slide, T-test has the highest power when all of the assumptions are satisfied. while Mann-whitney U test has higher power than T-test if only T-test assumption are not well satisfied, in other hand it marginally lower power, Median test is the strongest if assumption from both of the test mentioned before are not satissfied. In my case, Man-Whitney U test will be the test that i believe the most, since it has the highest power compared to Median test and T-test that the assumption are not fulfilled.
